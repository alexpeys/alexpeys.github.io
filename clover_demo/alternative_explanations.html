1. Expert Consensus on Primary Drivers of Recent US Inflation

The 2021-2023 inflation surge is widely attributed to several key drivers by economists and institutions.

1.1 Primary Inflation Drivers

DriverDescriptionConsensus View
Federal SpendingPandemic stimulus/reliefMajor contributor, esp. 2022 peak
Supply Chain DisruptionsGlobal bottlenecksSignificant early driver, diminished later
Energy PricesOil/gas volatility (esp. post-Ukraine war)Major contributor, esp. during peak
Labor Market TightnessWorker shortages, wage pressuresMore significant as other factors receded
Monetary PolicyLow rates, QESignificant underlying factor
Shift in Consumer DemandServices to goods during lockdownsImportant early contributor
Housing/Shelter CostsPersistent rent/price increasesDominant as other factors moderated

1.2 Evolution of Inflation Drivers Over Time

  • Early Phase (2021): Supply chains, energy, goods inflation dominated.
  • Peak Phase (2022): Federal spending, energy, food prices were primary.
  • Later Phase (2023-2025): Services (esp. housing) became dominant.

3. Potential Magnitude of Clover's Impact Against Other Factors

3.1 Clover's Market Position

Clover is a significant POS system but holds only a portion of the total payment processing market.

3.2 Comparative Analysis

The estimated impact of factors like federal spending (42%), energy (32-38%), and supply chains (~60% early on) vastly outweighs any plausible contribution from Clover specifically, or even the entire payment processing industry's fee changes.

3.3 Scale Comparison

  • Total US credit card processing fees (~$172B in 2023) are a fraction of pandemic federal spending (~$5T) or total US GDP (~$27T).
  • Fees are a distributed cost, not a concentrated shock like energy prices.

4. Confounding Variables

Factors that could create a false impression of Clover's impact:

  • Timing Coincidence: Clover's growth coincided with the inflation surge (Correlation â‰  Causation).
  • Visibility Bias: Processing fees are visible to merchants, potentially leading to overestimation of impact.
  • Pass-Through Perception: Merchants might cite fees for price increases driven by other costs.
  • Rising Spending: Higher overall spending increases absolute fee amounts even if rates are stable.
  • Sector Inflation: High inflation in key Clover sectors (e.g., restaurants) driven by unrelated factors (food/labor costs).

5. Counter-Arguments to Payment Processors Significantly Impacting Inflation

5.1 Economic Counter-Arguments

  1. Scale Mismatch: Total fees are too small relative to major drivers.
  2. Fee Stability (Relative): Average swipe fees (~1.8%) rose less dramatically than overall inflation (~20%) since pandemic start (Electronic Payments Coalition).
  3. Efficiency Gains: Processors like Clover might offset fees via increased efficiency.
  4. Pre-existing Cost: Processing fees existed long before the recent surge.
  5. Reverse Causality: Inflation raises nominal transaction values, increasing absolute fees.

5.2 Empirical Evidence

  1. Absence from Major Studies: Not identified as a significant driver by Fed, World Bank, IMF, etc.
  2. International Comparison: Similar inflation patterns occurred globally despite different payment systems.
  3. Sectoral Analysis: Inflation varied significantly across sectors without clear correlation to processor adoption.
  4. Historical Precedent: High inflation occurred previously (e.g., 1970s) with different payment systems.

6. Conclusion: Relative Importance of Different Inflation Factors

The evidence strongly indicates:

  1. Primary drivers of 2021-2023 inflation were federal spending, supply chain issues, energy prices, and demand shifts.
  2. Drivers later shifted towards services (esp. housing) and labor market tightness.
  3. Payment processing fees (general or Clover-specific) are not cited as significant contributors in major economic analyses.
  4. While merchants face processing costs, macroeconomic evidence doesn't support these fees as a meaningful driver of the broad inflation trend compared to larger economic forces.
  5. Correlation between Clover's growth and inflation appears coincidental.

In summary, alternative explanations involving large-scale fiscal/monetary policy, global supply/demand shocks, and specific market dynamics (energy, housing, labor) overwhelmingly account for the recent US inflation experience, with payment processing fees playing a negligible role in comparison.
