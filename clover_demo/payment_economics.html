1. Introduction

This literature review examines the theoretical and empirical relationship between payment processing systems and inflation, focusing on how processors like Clover might influence consumer prices by synthesizing academic research, industry reports, and expert opinions.

2. Economic Theory: Payment Networks as Two-Sided Markets

2.1 Two-Sided Market Framework

Payment card systems operate as two-sided markets connecting consumers and merchants via an intermediary platform (the payment network). These markets feature network externalities where each group's decisions affect the other (Rysman, 2009). Successful systems require both consumer usage and merchant acceptance.

2.2 Pricing Mechanisms in Payment Networks

Payment networks often use asymmetric pricing, subsidizing consumers (rewards) while charging merchants higher fees (interchange). This aims to maximize participation but can distort cost distribution.

3. Empirical Research: Payment Processing Fees and Consumer Prices

3.1 Merchant Cost Pass-Through

Federal Reserve Bank of Boston research (Felt et al., 2020) indicates merchants typically pass payment acceptance costs to all consumers via higher retail prices, regardless of the payment method used, even though credit cards are costlier for merchants than debit or cash.

3.2 Cross-Subsidization Effects

This behavior leads to cross-subsidization, where low-cost payment users (cash/debit) subsidize high-cost credit card users (especially rewards cards).

3.3 Regressive Distributional Effects

The Boston Fed found this system has regressive distributional effects: lower-income consumers pay the highest net cost as a percentage of transaction value, while higher-income consumers pay the lowest.

4. Merchant Behavior: Response to Processing Fees

4.1 Fee Absorption vs. Pass-Through

While some absorption may occur, research and industry data (Merchant Payments Coalition, 2024) suggest most merchants pass on processing fees (often their second-largest expense after labor) through higher prices.

4.2 Inflation Amplification Mechanism

Increased processing fees lead merchants to raise prices, potentially amplifying inflation (Clearly Payments, 2022). Price stabilization across the value chain is needed to curb this.

5. Case Studies: Price Effects of Payment Processing Changes

5.1 Rising Interchange Fees and Consumer Prices

Recent data (USA Today, 2024) show swipe fees increased 50% since the pandemic (vs. ~20% consumer price rise), hitting a record $172 billion in 2023. These costs are reflected in higher consumer prices.

5.2 Household Impact

The National Retail Federation estimates swipe fees cost the average household over $1,100 annually (up from $900 in 2021), a significant inflationary burden.

6. Expert Opinions on Payment Networks and Inflation

6.1 Industry Perspectives

Industry experts (e.g., Stephanie Martz, Merchant Payments Coalition) note that swipe fees continue rising even as general inflation subsides, suggesting they contribute to ongoing price pressures.

6.2 Regulatory Considerations

The Federal Reserve conducts ongoing research on interchange fees, reflecting regulatory concern about their potential economic impacts, including contributions to inflation.

7. Synthesis: Payment Processing Systems and Inflation

7.1 Mechanisms of Inflationary Impact

Payment systems may contribute to inflation via:

  1. Direct price increases: Merchant pass-through of fees.
  2. Cross-subsidization: Raising overall price levels.
  3. Fee escalation: Fees rising faster than general inflation.
  4. Regressive effects: Disproportionate impact on lower-income consumers.

7.2 Specific Evidence Regarding Clover

No specific empirical studies on Clover's direct contribution were found, but the general mechanisms apply. As a major player, Clover's fee structure and adoption contribute to the overall effects documented for the broader industry.

8. Conclusion

Substantial evidence suggests payment processing systems and fees contribute to consumer prices and potentially inflation. The two-sided market structure and merchant pass-through behavior mean fees are ultimately reflected in higher prices for all consumers. As fees have risen faster than general inflation recently, their impact may be growing. While specific research on Clover is limited, the general mechanisms apply. Further research quantifying Clover's specific contribution would be valuable.
