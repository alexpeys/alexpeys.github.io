Executive Summary

This section analyzes US inflation trends over the past decade, focusing on the surge beginning in 2021. It examines the peak, affected sectors, and primary causes according to expert analyses, providing an overview of CPI data, sectoral breakdowns, and contributing factors.

Inflation Surge and Peak

The post-pandemic inflation surge began in mid-2021, peaking in June 2022 at 9.1% (highest in over 40 years). Since then, inflation has generally declined, reaching 2.4% by March 2025, nearing the Federal Reserve's 2% target.

2. Sectoral Breakdown of Inflation

Food Inflation

  • Peaked at 11.4% in August 2022 (highest since May 1979).
  • As of March 2025, up 3.0% year-over-year.
  • Accounted for ~17% of overall inflation recently.

Energy Inflation

  • Highly volatile; major driver during 2021-2022 peak.
  • Contributed ~32% to overall inflation in June 2022.
  • As of March 2025, prices decreased 3.3% year-over-year.

Housing/Shelter Inflation

  • Became the dominant driver, accounting for ~60% of recent inflation increases.
  • Remained persistently high even as other sectors moderated.

Services vs. Goods Inflation

  • Shift from goods inflation (58% of peak) to services inflation (98% recently).
  • Core services inflation (excluding energy) has been persistent.

Other Notable Sectoral Trends (March 2025)

  • Instant coffee: +13.2%

  • Video subscription/rental: +10.7%
  • Uncooked ground beef: +10.4%

3. Primary Causes of Recent US Inflation

Expert analyses attribute the 2021-2023 inflation surge to multiple factors:

Pandemic-Related Disruptions

  • Supply Chain Disruptions
  • Labor Market Disruptions
  • Shift in Consumer Demand (Goods vs. Services)

Fiscal and Monetary Policy

  • Fiscal Stimulus Packages
  • Accommodative Monetary Policy (Low rates, QE)
  • Expanded Money Supply

Global Factors

  • Russia-Ukraine War (Energy, Food)
  • Global Commodity Prices
  • International Supply Chain Issues

Structural Factors

  • Pre-existing Housing Shortages
  • Climate Impacts
  • Government Budget Deficits

4. Expert Analysis on Inflation Drivers

Federal Reserve Analysis

Initially viewed inflation as "transitory" (supply bottlenecks, demand surge, stimulus checks). Later recognized more persistent factors (supply contractions, demand increases, idiosyncratic factors).

Academic and Economic Research

  • MIT Sloan emphasized federal spending as the "overwhelming driver" in 2022.
  • Other studies highlighted supply chain disruptions.
  • Some analyses pointed to the combination of expansionary fiscal and monetary policies.

5. Current State and Outlook (March 2025)

  • Overall inflation: 2.4%
  • Core inflation (excl. food/energy): 2.8%
  • Services inflation remains primary driver (98% of overall).
  • Shelter costs are the largest contributor (60%).

The Federal Reserve has been gradually reducing interest rates, but concerns remain about potential resurgence (e.g., tariffs, trade tensions).

Conclusion

The US inflation experience of 2021-2025 resulted from a complex interplay of pandemic disruptions, policy responses, and global events. While peak inflation has moderated significantly, the primary drivers shifted from goods and energy to services and housing. This analysis highlights inflation as a multifaceted challenge with varying impacts across the economy.
